How much do we know about the size of the Earth?

The Greek philosopher Aristotle (384-322 B.C.) is credited as the first person to try and calculate the size of the Earth by determining its circumference (the length around the equator)

But how big is the Earth, really? If only about 5% of the ocean has been explored, and the seven continents take up 149 million square kilometers (57 million square miles), where are we getting the rest of the surface area?

"The total surface area of Earth is about 197 million square miles (510 million square km). About 71% of our planet is covered by water and 29% by land," according to Space.com's article, 'How big is Earth?'

What is the Earth really made of?

The Earth's structure, according to Geological Survey Ireland, "is made up of three different layers: the crust, the mantle and the core."

If the oceans are 95% unexplored, how do we know how far down they go? How do we know how big the Earth's crust is, or whether there is one or not?

The inner core of the Earth is supposedly made of iron and nickel metals. The crust is made up of "mostly oxygen, silicon, aluminum, iron, calcium, sodium, potassium and magnesium," states the Cool Cosmos 'What is Earth made of?' page.

This idea that the Earth is made of nickel and lead and all the other metals on the periodic table is a questionable one at best.
